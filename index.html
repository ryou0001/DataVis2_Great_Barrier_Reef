Tensions of the Great Barrier Reef Region

Residents' concerns for the preservation of the Great Barrier Reef exist in tension with the economic realities of the area.

The Great Barrier Reef Region describes an area that includes the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park and six Natural Resource Management (NRM) Regions.

Most residents of the Great Barrier Reef Region live in population centres along the Queensland coastline. Cairns and Townsville are the largest of these centres.

A survey conducted in 2017 sought to find out residents' feelings both about the Great Barrier Reef, and threats to the Reef.


Residents tended to speak positively of the Great Barrier Reef, with the overwhelming majority strongly agreeing with the statement "I love that I live beside the GBR".

More than 94% of residents had visited the Great Barrier Reef before, and more than 87% had visited at least once in the previous twelve months.

When residents were asked to list the first words that come to mind when they think of the Great Barrier Reef, the most common words were positive adjectives such as beautiful and beauty, as well as nouns associated with the reef such as coral and fish.

However, it was also common for residents to report more negative words and phrases such as bleaching and endangered. Many residents also gave the words tourism and tourist.

By looking at phrases, certain patterns become clear. For example, coral is a common word as residents associate the neutral nouns coral and corals with the reef, but also the negative event coral bleaching.


Jobs in the Great Barrier Reef region tend to follow a similar pattern to jobs in Australia. No one job type goes beyond 12% of the region's jobs. Australia's top 3 most common job types, Health care and social assistance, Retail trade, and Education and training all appear as top 5 most common job types for the region. Accommodation and food services and Construction also appear in Australia's top 10 most common job types.

However, when two less common job types are examined - mining and tourism - the tensions of the region become clear.



Mining


Almost thirty thousand jobs in the Great Barrier Reef Region are in mining, comprising more than 4% of total jobs. This may seem like a small percentage, but mining jobs make up less than 2% of jobs in Queensland, and an even smaller percantage of jobs in Australia. Thus mining is an important employer in the region.

Contrast this with the fact that 16% of residents think that mining is a threat to the Great Barrier Reef.



Tourism


Sixty thousand jobs are supported by marine tourism.[1] If all these jobs are concentrated in the Great Barrier Reef region, that would mean more than 8% of total jobs are supported by tourism.

Tourism to the region is concentrated to 7% of the total Great Barrier Reef Marine Park. 86% of tourism occurs in the waters around Cairns, Port Douglas, and the Whitsundays.[1]

Residents are concerned that tourism is a threat to the Great Barrier Reef. Understandably, areas that see more tourists tended to have higher concern.

The Great Barrier Reef Marine Park is divided into four managment areas, that do not correspond to the NRM Regions in which residents live. Tourist data is broken down by the management areas. Cairns/Cooktown, which includes the waters around Cairns, and Townsville/Whitsunday, which includes the waters around the Whitsundays, are the most popular tourist destinations.

The residents' survey occurred in 2017. Since then, Queensland closed its borders twice in response to the global pandemic. During the closures, tourism numbers dropped. Tourist visits have not yet returned to pre-pandemic numbers.



Climate Change


Residents listed climate change as one of their biggest concerns, second only to pollution.

Climate change is indeed threatening the Great Barrier Reef, as rising sea water temperatures are causing mass coral bleaching events. Multiple Great Barrier Reef bleaching events have occurred in the past ten years, including in 2016, 2017, 2020, and 2022. Each mass bleaching event corresponds to higher than average ocean temperatures.
